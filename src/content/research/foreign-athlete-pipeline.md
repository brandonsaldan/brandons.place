This paper argues that the foreign athlete pipeline in American college athletics is not a neutral market mechanism but a political economy institution embedded in the international system. Ten timeline entries spanning 1952 to 2026 trace how the pipeline was shaped by Cold War soft power logic, hardened by post-9/11 immigration enforcement, implicated in Chinese state talent acquisition operations on American campuses, and further complicated by the NCAA's shift to direct athlete compensation under the House v. NCAA settlement. The paper applies dependency theory, securitization theory, and soft power frameworks to argue that the same institutional openness that enabled American universities to extract athletic labor from peripheral nations also made those universities vulnerable to exploitation by foreign state actors, two structurally distinct consequences of the same underlying governance failure.

Produced as part of undergraduate coursework at the University of North Carolina at Charlotte in AMST 3050: History of the NCAA.
